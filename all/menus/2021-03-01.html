Menu for Week of March 1, 2021

The themes for this week are continuing to use up the winter stuff we have and trialing some new cookbooks.

Looking around the root cellar, it's becoming very clear what we still have too much of and what we need to make more of next summer. We're running low on plain whole or crushed tomatoes, while the number of squash we still have is ... a little daunting. So, menus for the next couple of months will have to reflect that, although I'm not sure the squash, not to mention the remaining apples, can go much longer without becoming moldy or otherwise unsuitable. I would say we have just about the right amounts of onions and potatoes, though.

I am going to ferment some garlic this week which will help get us through from now (when it is on the verge of going bad) to the time when fresh garlic starts coming in again (usually July). Some of the hardneck garlic has sprouted, so we're setting aside those cloves to plant outside. I know spring isn't the right time to plant garlic, really, but I think we will get at least some harvest out of it? So I've heard anyway.

It is time to start thinking about the garden again. For this year, I think our gardening will be limited, because we have a terrible slug problem and we lost so many plants and vegetables to slugs last year. We tried all sorts of remedies for this and nothing seems to have really worked. So this year we're only going to plant sparsely and only plants that the slugs don't seem to bother, such as radishes and squash.

OK, so onto the menu for this week. I have recently bought a few cookbooks that are aimed at less-experienced cooks. One of these is Cookish by Christopher Kimball and the crew at Milk Street. Another is Small Victories by Julia Turshen. In some way it might also be reasonable to see Eat by Nigel Slater in a similar category, and also Alison Roman's excellent Dining In. One of my goals this year is to encourage the other members of the household to more independence in the kitchen; whenever I can't cook for some reason (illness, depression, social engagement), there's a bit of a problem because no one else knows what we have or where to find a recipe that might suitably use ingredients we have or even, and this has surprised me somewhat, how to read the recipes they might find. What we're learning about reading recipes itself deserves its own post, but that will have to wait.

So, in the coming weeks we'll be doing some "better know a cookbook" nights. What I want to do is learn what questions the different cooks in the household have about these recipes: what is easy to mess up, because the instruction isn't clear enough? how well can they understand from the accompanying text and descriptions what the end result should be like?

Onward!

Spaghetti squash time!

We should write the spaghetti squash pizza boats up into a proper recipe sometime, but for now:

Bake spaghetti squash until quite tender, about one hour. Meanwhile, brown a pound of Italian sausage (sweet or hot) in a little oil. If you're using mushrooms, add them after the meat is browned, or, if you're using mushrooms instead of the meat, brown them in some oil. Once the meat and/or mushrooms is browned, add several minced cloves of garlic and a couple of tablespoons of tomato paste and saute as the tomato paste becomes darker red and the garlic becomes fragrant. Add a pint of crushed tomatoes plus a bunch of dried herbs or use a pint of spaghetti sauce. Next year we might can pizza sauce instead of spaghetti sauce and then we might not need the tomato paste here, but anyway. Cook until it's reduced and very thick. You can add more herbs, some paprika, some chopped peppers or olives to the sauce as it cooks, and that is fine. You want this sauce to be very thick, tangy, salty, and savory, because the squash is more watery and sweet than pasta is, so you need to offset that somewhat by making your sauce stronger flavored than a normal pasta or pizza sauce might be. Take the "spaghetti" out of the squash: i.e., cut the squash open (in half, if you want to serve these as "boats"), scoop out the seeds and the gunk connected to the seeds, and then loosen the meat from the skin of the squash. I like to use a fork to do this. Scrape the strands of squash flesh into your thickened sauce and mix well until the strands are all coated. If your squash skins are still intact and you want "boats", put the sauced "spaghetti" back into the skins. Sometimes our squash skins get too damaged when we're scraping out the flesh and we just bake this like a casserole instead of in the skins. You can add more pizza toppings if you want: add some pepperoni, add some anchovies, add more vegetables, either on top of the "noodles" or in the sauce. Then top with a bunch of cheese, inside the squash skins or in a casserole, and bake until the cheese is melted and the whole thing looks bubbly.

Of course, we don't have fresh bok choy as the chili-soy noodles recipe asks for, but we have frozen vegetables; we will probably use frozen broccoli.

Kabocha salad is something I used to eat and make in Japan a lot, and I don't really have a recipe. Basically, peel a kabocha (or mostly peel it; a few little flecks of green peel are nice left on it for both extra color and texture), cut it into bite-size chunks, and simmer or steam it until barely tender. Mix it in a bowl with Kewpie mayonnaise — yes, it has to be Japanese mayonnaise, I'm sorry — and cucumber -- in Japan, where I had nearly year-round greenhouse cucumbers, I would use fresh, but here, where I don't, I use honey-garlic pickles that I made in the summer. They're already sliced when I make the pickle, too, making this very convenient, and their very slight sweetness is perfect for this salad.

Friday

I sort of feel like this could use another vegetable but I'm tired, we're all tired, and so I think we'll just eat canned peaches for dessert or something.

  • Pork chops with salsa macha from Cookish, made by me

  • Kinda sorta patatas bravas from Small Victories, made by my younger son

  • Zucchini and nigella fritters from Small Victories, made by Chris

The zucchini for the fritters is shredded zucchini that I fermented last summer. You can also shred zucchini and then freeze it for breads or for fritters like this, and it works out pretty well. I've done that for many years, but two summers ago I started fermenting it instead. I don't think either is particularly superior; since we never eat this fermented shredded zucchini raw, we don't ever end up getting the benefits of eating fermented foods from it. However, it is convenient that we don't have to thaw it before using it in zucchini bread or muffins or fritters.

I bought nigella seeds a while back but had never tasted them, so this will be a nice opportunity to try them out. If we like them, maybe we will try to grow some on our own. I love it when we can grow a beautiful flower that has also some delicious uses.
