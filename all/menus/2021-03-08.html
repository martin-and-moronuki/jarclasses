This week we'll still be doing the "better know a cookbook" thing, working with the same set of cookbooks as last week. So far, this has been really successful, and I think it's just a big help to us as a family to have these cookbooks here and find how accessible they are to all of us.

I'm getting into the habit of making kombucha now, and I love it. I've had some problems getting it as fizzy as I like it, which I think are due to not having jars or bottles for the secondary fermentation that are airtight enough. I didn't want to invest in more bottles until I knew I was going to keep up the habit, but I'm finding that having kombucha around more is something I want and that the process fits pretty easily into my weekend rhythm with making bread and everything. We'll see if I can maintain it in the summers. I don't bake our bread during the summers usually, at least not on a weekly basis; we have a bread subscription from a local bakery through our CSA and we also buy breads from local bakers, at the farmer's market or occasional trips to the Dixon Mercantile. We have a "floating" share in the Glacier Tilth CSA in Dixon anyway, so I'm often in Dixon on the weekends.

Monday

Vaguely, but only very vaguely, "Asian." I don't really like calling food "Asian" because, come on, there's basically nothing in common between, like, Japanese food and Thai food, but nevertheless we are having a bunch of flavors from that largest of continents here. They don't really go together, this isn't a totally coherent artistic statement, but it's fine.

  • Turmeric Chicken with asparagus

  • Rice plus we'll make extra for fried rice later in the week

  • Red curry kuri soup

  • Parsnip kinpira

The turmeric chicken is a recipe that was popular among people I follow on Twitter last spring, and I'm craving it and we have frozen asparagus still, which will not be the same but might still be good. I also got the idea for the red curry kuri soup from a blog, but I'm going to make it differently; nevertheless, it will be a soup made with red kuri squash and Thai red curry paste. If it's good, we'll eventually post a recipe for it. Kinpira is traditionally made with burdock root and carrot, but here we'll substitute parsnip for the burdock, to use up the parsnips that are currently oppressing us with their overwhelming numbers.

Do I even need to say that the cherries are frozen from last summer?

One of the things Chris and I have talked about in terms of him learning how to cook more is that I am often choosing to make completely different things from night to night, so he doesn't feel he gets the repeated exposure to the same dish he needs in order to master it. This is a very sensible remark. Last week, he made zucchini fritters; tonight, he's going to make fritters but with root vegetables instead. I'm hoping this reinforcement will give us a chance to note the similarities between the two recipes. You can bind vegetables with some egg and flour and make them into little patties and shallow-fry them and they will be delicious, almost always. You can also, of course, do this with, e.g., canned tuna, and it is still delicious. So many options in the fritter family.

I don't know if we've mentioned that we grow bean sprouts in a mason jar on the counter during the winter. They're such a delightful and easy source of fresh vegetables during the winter, and so we eat a bean sprouts dish (usually, I have to admit, it's egg foo yung, because I am a serious fan of egg foo yung, either with the brown gravy or on a St. Paul sandwich) about every ten days. This day it will be a classic Korean banchan; I don't particularly need a recipe for this, but there's a good one in the Koreatown cookbook. The corn cheese recipe is from the Lucky Peach 101 Easy Asian Recipes cookbook, as solid an introduction to easy Asian foods for home cooks as I've seen; Asian fits here, because it features typical homecooking-style recipes from many different Asian countries as well as some Asian-American dishes that, let's face it, have ties to something vaguely Asian but are clearly not traditional or authentic such as the St. Paul sandwich. Sorry to have brought that sandwich up two times in a single paragraph.

Anyway, corn cheese, as they note in the cookbook, is a frequent bar snack apparently in Korea but I've eaten a very similar thing in many Japanese bars as well, and I'm craving it. It is ... sort of gross? But also good with beer. And it calls for frozen corn, not fresh, so we're all good there.

Sunday

I'm feeling the big Sunday dinner thing here, which usually means either fried or roasted chicken or a big pot of baked beans or sometimes beans and chicken. But this week I think it will be beans and ribs.

  • Pork ribs, baked in the oven

  • Boston style baked beans served with (home canned) Indian pickle

  • Ranch cole slaw

I use the recipe for baking ribs in The Joy of Cooking as I find it very reliable. I don't like mine with sauce, just a flavorful rub on them. I never order ribs in restaurants and thought I didn't like them until I started making them at home; it turns out, I just don't really like sauce on them. I realize there are probably lots of great BBQ joints where you can get perfect ribs with no sauce, but I didn't know this, and I always assumed that they would have sauce (even though the other BBQ meats don't at such places) so I never tried. Don't @ me.

You have to have cole slaw with such a meal, in my opinion. Here we will make a cole slaw with a ranch style dressing and we will incorporate some sauerkraut into it. My younger son doesn't like sour foods much, and so he rarely wants to eat the sauerkrauts I make, but we have found that if we stir them into a cole slaw, about half and half with raw cabbage, he doesn't notice the sourness. It's a nice trick for using up sauerkrauts while still getting the whole gut-bacteria benefit from eating them — what I mean is lots of other tricks involve cooking the fermented things, which destroys the bacteria, but this one involves eating them raw, just hidden.
